## 1. Programme Overview

This documentation covers a six-month end-to-end commercial mushroom cultivation programme conducted at SKUAST Jammu, covering two edible fungal species: *Agaricus bisporus* (button mushroom) and *Pleurotus ostreatus* (oyster mushroom). The programme spanned all stages of commercial production — from axenic culture development in the laboratory through to harvest, yield analysis, disease management, and full financial accounting.

The programme was designed to simulate real-world commercial production conditions, with each student team responsible for all decisions, records, and outcomes across two complete production cycles per species.

**Programme objectives:**
- Develop end-to-end commercial mushroom production competency
- Maintain structured production records across all stages
- Achieve target biological efficiency benchmarks
- Manage contamination events using established protocols
- Conduct full cost-benefit analysis across the production run

## 2. Species & Substrate Selection

### *Agaricus bisporus* (Button Mushroom)
- **Substrate:** Composted wheat straw with poultry manure amendments
- **Compost weight per bag:** 10 kg
- **Number of bags:** 15
- **Casing layer:** Peat-lime mix (pH adjusted to 7.2–7.5)
- **Target biological efficiency:** 18–25%

### *Pleurotus ostreatus* (Oyster Mushroom)
- **Substrate:** Wheat straw (pasteurised)
- **Substrate weight per bag:** 5 kg
- **Number of bags:** 10
- **No casing layer required**
- **Target biological efficiency:** 25–40%

**Substrate selection rationale:** Wheat straw was selected as the primary substrate base for both species due to local availability, low cost, and established performance data. Button mushroom compost was additionally supplemented with nitrogen-rich amendments to support the more demanding nutritional requirements of *A. bisporus*.

## 3. Laboratory Setup & Sterilisation Protocols

All spawn production and culture work was conducted under strict aseptic conditions using the following laboratory infrastructure:

| Equipment | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| Laminar Air Flow (LAF) Cabinet | Aseptic transfer, inoculation, and culture work |
| Autoclave | Sterilisation of media, grain, and equipment |
| BOD Incubator | Controlled temperature incubation (25°C for *Pleurotus*, 24°C for *Agaricus*) |
| Refrigerator | Short-term culture storage (4°C) |
| Boiling vessel | Grain pre-treatment before sterilisation |
| Standard glassware | Media preparation, reagent handling |
| Polypropylene bags | Substrate and spawn containers |

### Sterilisation Protocol — Culture Media
1. Prepare media (PDA or MEA) according to standard formulation
2. Dispense into culture bottles or Petri dishes
3. Autoclave at 121°C, 15 psi for 20 minutes
4. Allow to cool in LAF cabinet before use
5. Store at 4°C if not used immediately

### Sterilisation Protocol — Grain Substrate (for Spawn)
1. Soak wheat grains in water for 12 hours
2. Boil for 20–25 minutes until grains are soft but not burst
3. Drain and surface-dry grains
4. Fill polypropylene bags to two-thirds capacity
5. Autoclave at 121°C, 15 psi for 2.5 hours
6. Cool completely before inoculation

## 4. Mother Spawn Production

Mother spawn was produced through two parallel methods: tissue culture and spore culture. Both methods were conducted under full aseptic conditions inside the LAF cabinet.

### 4.1 Tissue Culture Method

**Objective:** Isolate pure mycelial culture from a healthy fruiting body for use as a starter culture.

**Protocol:**
1. Select a firm, healthy, disease-free fruiting body
2. Surface-sterilise with 70% ethanol
3. Flame-sterilise scalpel; allow to cool briefly
4. Tear (do not cut) the fruiting body to expose internal tissue
5. Excise a small piece (~3–5 mm) from the junction of cap and stipe
6. Transfer to PDA plate immediately inside LAF cabinet
7. Seal plates with Parafilm; incubate at 25°C
8. Observe daily for mycelial growth (visible from day 3–5)
9. Sub-culture onto fresh PDA upon confirmed clean growth

**Culture media used:**
- **PDA (Potato Dextrose Agar):** 200g potato (boiled extract), 20g dextrose, 20g agar per litre
- **MEA (Malt Extract Agar):** 20g malt extract, 20g agar per litre

### 4.2 Spore Culture Method

1. Place a fresh, mature cap gill-side down on sterile paper inside LAF cabinet
2. Cover with a sterile glass dish; leave for 4–6 hours for spore print
3. Transfer spore print to PDA using sterile inoculation loop
4. Incubate at 25°C; observe for germination

### 4.3 Transfer to Grain — Mother Spawn

Once clean mycelial growth was confirmed on PDA (approximately 7–10 days):
1. Cut small plugs of colonised agar (approximately 1 cm²)
2. Transfer 3–4 plugs per bottle of sterile grain inside LAF cabinet
3. Seal bottles; incubate at 25°C
4. Shake bottles every 2–3 days to distribute mycelium through grain
5. Full colonisation achieved in approximately 14–18 days
6. Mother spawn stored at 4°C until required for scale-up

## 5. Commercial Spawn Scale-Up

Mother spawn was used to inoculate large volumes of sterilised grain for commercial-scale spawn production.

**Scale-up ratio:** 1 bottle mother spawn : 10 bottles commercial spawn

**Protocol:**
1. Prepare and sterilise grain as per Section 3
2. Allow to cool completely (below 30°C before inoculation)
3. In LAF cabinet, transfer 2–3 tablespoons of mother spawn per bag of commercial grain
4. Seal bags; incubate at 25°C
5. Monitor daily; shake every 2–3 days
6. Full colonisation: 18–22 days
7. Commercial spawn ready for use or storage at 4°C (viable for up to 30 days)

**Quality check before use:**
- Uniform white mycelial growth throughout grain
- Pleasant mushroom odour
- No green, black, or pink discolouration
- No sour or off-odour

## 6. Substrate Preparation & Bag Filling

### *Agaricus bisporus* — Compost Preparation
1. Layer wheat straw with poultry manure amendments
2. Wet to 65–70% moisture content (press test: water should just not drip)
3. Form windrow; turn every 3–4 days for 25–28 days (Phase I composting)
4. Peak heating phase: internal temperature reaches 70–80°C (pasteurisation achieved)
5. Fill 10 kg polypropylene bags with finished compost
6. Allow to cool before spawn addition

### *Pleurotus ostreatus* — Substrate Preparation
1. Chop wheat straw to 3–5 cm lengths
2. Soak in water for 12 hours
3. Pasteurise in hot water at 80°C for 1.5–2 hours
4. Drain and cool to below 30°C
5. Fill 5 kg polypropylene bags in layers

### Spawn Inoculation
- **Button mushroom:** Spawn mixed at 2–3% of compost weight; mixed uniformly throughout bag
- **Oyster mushroom:** Spawn applied in layers between substrate; approximately 100–120g spawn per 5 kg bag
- Bags sealed; incubation at 24–25°C in darkness for full colonisation (18–25 days)

## 7. Cropping & Yield Management

### Button Mushroom (*A. bisporus*)
- Casing layer applied after full colonisation (peat-lime mix, 4–5 cm depth)
- Temperature reduced to 16–18°C to initiate pinning
- Relative humidity maintained at 85–90%
- First flush appeared at approximately day 18–22 post-casing
- Mushrooms harvested at button stage (before veil breaks) by twisting and pulling
- Two complete production cycles conducted

### Oyster Mushroom (*P. ostreatus*)
- Bags slashed at 5–6 points after full colonisation to allow fruiting body emergence
- Temperature maintained at 20–24°C; humidity at 80–90%
- Fresh air exchange: 4–6 air changes per hour
- First flush appeared at approximately day 7–10 post-slashing
- Harvested by pulling entire cluster at base before spore release
- Two complete production cycles conducted

## 8. Disease Identification & Management

Disease management was one of the most practically instructive components of the programme. Two contamination events occurred across the production run.

### 8.1 Green Mould — *Trichoderma* spp.

**Identification:** Rapid-spreading green powdery growth, typically appearing first at bag openings or damaged areas. Distinct musty odour.

**Cause in this programme:** Insufficient sterilisation time on one batch of grain spawn; identified as the source through batch-tracing of affected bags.

**Management actions taken:**
1. Affected bags immediately isolated and removed from cropping room
2. Surrounding surfaces treated with 2% formalin solution
3. Room temperature temporarily reduced to slow spread
4. Carbendazim (0.1% solution) applied to substrate surface of at-risk adjacent bags
5. Ventilation increased to reduce humidity in affected zone
6. All personnel required to change gloves before handling unaffected bags

**Outcome:** Contamination contained to 2 bags out of 15 (button mushroom batch). Remaining bags unaffected.

### 8.2 White Mould — *Lecanicillium* spp.

**Identification:** White fluffy growth, visually similar to healthy mycelium but with softer texture and slight yellowing of affected substrate.

**Management actions taken:**
1. Affected areas physically removed and bagged for disposal
2. Formalin treatment applied to affected surfaces
3. Temperature reduced by 2°C across cropping room
4. Increased monitoring frequency to daily inspection

**Outcome:** Contained to surface layer only; underlying substrate unaffected; yield from affected bags reduced but not eliminated.

### Preventative Protocols Maintained Throughout
- All equipment flame-sterilised before use
- LAF cabinet UV lamp run for 30 minutes before each work session
- Footbaths with disinfectant at cropping room entrance
- Records maintained of all contamination events, locations, and responses

## 9. Culture Preservation

Pure cultures were preserved using three methods to ensure long-term viability and backup security.

| Method | Temperature | Viability | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Refrigeration (short-term) | 4°C | 3–6 months | PDA slants; regular sub-culturing required |
| Freezing | -20°C | 12–24 months | Cultures in 20% glycerol solution |
| Cryogenic freezing | -80°C | 5+ years | In 10% DMSO cryoprotectant; most reliable long-term method |
| Freeze-drying (lyophilisation) | N/A | 10+ years | Cultures desiccated under vacuum; gold standard for long-term preservation |

All preserved cultures were labelled with species, strain, date of preservation, and method. Records maintained in a culture preservation log.

## 10. Spent Substrate Recycling

Spent mushroom substrate (SMS) was not discarded after the production programme. Recomposting and recycling protocols were implemented:

1. SMS collected from all harvested bags after final flush
2. Analysed for residual nutrient content (visual and physical assessment)
3. Mixed with fresh agricultural waste (straw, manure) in a 1:1 ratio
4. Composted for 30 days with regular turning
5. Final compost assessed for moisture, texture, and odour
6. Recomposted material used as soil amendment for field plots within the university farm

**Environmental and economic rationale:** SMS recomposting reduced waste disposal costs, returned organic matter to the soil, and demonstrated closed-loop production thinking relevant to sustainable agricultural operations.

## 13. Key Learnings & Observations

**On documentation:**
Maintaining consistent, accurate records across two species and two production cycles was more demanding than anticipated. Small record-keeping gaps early in the programme created compounding issues when reconstructing data for financial analysis. This reinforced the value of real-time documentation over retrospective recall — a principle directly applicable to any regulatory or compliance documentation environment.

**On contamination management:**
The *Trichoderma* outbreak in Cycle 1 of button mushroom production was the most instructive event of the programme. Batch-tracing the source to a specific autoclave run underloaded with grain highlighted how a single process deviation can have downstream production consequences. Systematic documentation of each production step was what made the root cause traceable.

**On biological efficiency:**
Oyster mushroom consistently outperformed button mushroom in biological efficiency across both cycles. The gap between Cycle 1 and Cycle 2 for both species suggests that operator competency and process consistency are significant variables — Cycle 2 performance improved in every metric measured, which is consistent with the learning curve effect observed in commercial mushroom operations.

**On cost-benefit analysis:**
The BCR of 1.48 (conservative blended estimate) was achieved under training conditions with sub-optimal infrastructure and without economies of scale. Commercial operations with proper environmental controls, higher spawn rates, and optimised substrate formulations routinely achieve BCRs of 2.0–3.5. The programme demonstrated financial viability even at small scale with limited inputs.
